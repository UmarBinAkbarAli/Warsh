## 1. The short version (say this first)

We named the app **Warsh · وَرْش** after one of the two great transmissions of the Holy Quran — *Warsh ʿan Nāfiʿ*.

But behind the transmission is a person: a young Egyptian named **ʿUthmān ibn Saʿīd**, who left home, traveled to Medina, and sat at the feet of a master until he had received the Quran the way it was meant to be received — directly, carefully, from teacher to student. His teacher loved him and gave him a warm nickname: **Warsh**. That nickname outlived the man. Today, more than 1,200 years later, millions of Muslims recite the Quran in the way that carries his name.

That is who we want to be for our learner. Warsh is the app for the person who doesn't just want to recite — they want to *understand*, the way that young student in Medina did. We are the teacher who receives them, the journey that takes them there, and the name that stays with them long after.

## 2. Who Warsh was (the man behind the name)

His real name was **ʿUthmān ibn Saʿīd al-Qibṭī al-Miṣrī** — *al-Miṣrī* meaning "the Egyptian." He was born in Egypt around **110 AH (c. 728 CE)** and passed away around **197 AH (c. 812 CE)**.

As a young man, he did something that defines our whole brand: **he traveled to seek knowledge.** He left Egypt and went to **Medina** specifically to study the recitation of the Quran under **Imām Nāfiʿ al-Madanī** — one of the famous *Seven Reciters*, the leading authority on Quranic recitation in the city of the Prophet ﷺ.

He didn't learn from a book. He learned **mouth to ear, heart to heart** — the living chain of transmission (*sanad*) that connects every reciter back, link by link, to the Prophet ﷺ himself. He stayed, he mastered it, and his teacher came to know him well enough to give him an affectionate nickname.

When he had finished, **he went home.** He returned to Egypt and became the senior reciter (*qāriʾ*) of the land — the person others now traveled to learn from. The student had become the teacher.

His way of reciting, taken from Nāfiʿ, became known as **Warsh ʿan Nāfiʿ** — "Warsh, from Nāfiʿ." Alongside **Ḥafṣ ʿan ʿĀṣim**, it is one of the **two most widely used transmissions of the Quran in the entire Muslim world.** It is the dominant recitation across North and West Africa to this day.

**The arc in one line:** *He left home to learn, was loved by his teacher, mastered the craft, returned to teach, and his name became part of how the world recites the Quran.*

## 3. What the word means

The name **Warsh (وَرْش)** is a *laqab* — an affectionate nickname — not his given name and not the name of a recitation method.

His teacher, Imām Nāfiʿ, called him *Warsh* because of his **fair complexion and bright face.** The word itself refers to a **substance made from milk** — so the nickname carried a sense of something light, pure, and bright. It was given out of warmth, the way a beloved teacher might give a student a fond name that everyone then picks up.

Two things worth holding onto:

- **It came from a teacher's affection.** The name was born inside a teacher–student relationship. That is not a coincidence we are leaning on by accident — it is the emotional center of our whole product.
- **The nickname became bigger than the man.** A small, warm name given in a classroom in Medina is now spoken by millions. Names carry weight. They travel further than we expect.

> **One careful note for accuracy:** "Warsh" is the nickname of the *transmitter*, not the name of a different Quran. Warsh and Ḥafṣ recite the *same* Quran — the differences are in pronunciation, elongation, and certain readings, never in meaning. If anyone ever asks whether "Warsh is a different Quran," the answer is no. It is the same revelation, faithfully preserved through a different chain.

## 4. Why we chose it (the thinking)

We chose Warsh on purpose, and every reason maps onto something the app actually is.

**1. It plants us in the classical Quranic tradition — not modern, not dialect.**
The name immediately signals what we teach: the Arabic of the Quran. Not Khaleeji, not Levantine, not "travel Arabic." Someone who hears *Warsh* and knows the tradition understands our lane in a single word. This is what separates us from Duolingo Arabic, Kalam, and Alifbee before a user reads a single line of copy.

**2. It is built on a teacher–student relationship — and so are we.**
The name was given by a teacher to a student. Our entire product is built around **Ustaad Noor**, the warm, scholarly teacher who never shames and never gives hollow praise. The name and the product carry the same DNA: learning happens between a teacher who cares and a student who shows up.

**3. It honors the journey of *going to learn*.**
Warsh traveled to seek knowledge. Our learner is on the same journey — most of them have already watched the YouTube videos, felt the pull, and want to actually *arrive* somewhere. Warsh is what happens after the video. It is the road to understanding, not just admiring.

**4. It carries the idea of faithful transmission and preservation.**
The whole science of *qirāʾāt* exists to preserve the Quran exactly. We are doing a smaller version of the same thing: faithfully passing on the grammar, vocabulary, and meaning so that the learner can receive the Quran with comprehension and pass it on in turn.

**5. It is a name that outlived its owner — a brand built to last.**
A nickname from a classroom became a thousand-year legacy. That is the kind of permanence we are reaching for. We are not building a streak-chasing game; we are building something a person carries with them.

This is why our taglines fit the name so naturally:
- *"Where Arabic is crafted."*
- *"Grammar is worship. Learn it like one."*

Both say the same thing the name says: this is sacred, careful, classical work — done with warmth.

## 6. What this name commits us to

Choosing a name from the Quranic tradition is a responsibility, not just a label. By calling ourselves Warsh, we are promising:

- **Authenticity.** We teach the real classical Arabic of the Quran, sourced from real scholarship. No shortcuts that betray the tradition.
- **Warmth over shame.** The name was born from a teacher's affection. Ustaad Noor — and everything we write — must carry that same warmth. We never shame the learner.
- **Faithful transmission.** What we teach must be correct. We are passing something sacred down the chain.
- **A journey worth completing.** The name honors someone who *finished* — who mastered and returned to teach. We design for the learner who arrives, not just the one who starts.

> Every future brand decision — mission, vision, voice, story — should be able to answer one question:
> **"Is this worthy of the name?"**
