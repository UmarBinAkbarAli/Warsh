1 Hook ~30 sec Quran first
One Quranic ayah shown — no explanation. The user feels the gap: "I've read this 1000 times and I don't understand it." Creates the need before filling it. Never skip this step — the emotional anchor is the whole point.
Example: Show بِسْمِ اللهِ — ask "what does اسم mean here?"
2 Discover ~60 sec
3–4 example cards. Arabic large, translation below. No grammar labels — patterns only. User taps through at their pace. This is exposure, not testing. The brain is absorbing before being asked to produce.
Rule: Never introduce a grammar term here. Patterns only.
3 Practice 2–4 min min 3 formats
4–6 exercises using at least 3 different interaction formats. Each exercise gives immediate feedback. Correct: بارك الله فيك + explanation. Wrong: "Almost — let's look at this again" + correct answer shown. Never "Wrong!" Never silence.
Rule: True/False must appear at least once — it resets pacing.
4 Reveal ~30 sec Grammar named here only
Name the grammar concept they just learned — AFTER they've learned it. Show the classical Arabic term. Tell them madrasah students study this for months. Then show the original ayah again — now it makes sense.
This is the emotional peak. Never rush it. Never make it feel like a test result.
5 Close ~20 sec
Stats (pts earned, accuracy, words learned). Surah Al-Fatiha comprehension meter update. Ustaad Noor gives one specific tip — connects today's lesson to an ayah to look for tonight. Two buttons: Next lesson or View journey.
Noor never says "Great job!" He says "You're doing beautifully — this root alone unlocks 40 Quranic words."
8 interaction formats — when each is used
Flashcard Units 1, 2, 7
Arabic word front. Tap to flip — meaning, transliteration, example sentence revealed. Used for pure recognition: the user's job is just to see and remember the shape. No production required.
Letter flip Unit 1 only
Letter shape shown. Tap to hear sound. Swipe to see connected forms (start / middle / end / isolated). Only in Unit 1 — script recognition phase. Never used once letters are established.
Multiple choice Units 2–6
Arabic word or sentence shown. 4 meaning options. Brief explanation shown after answer — not just correct/wrong. This is the workhorse format. Used across all middle units.
Fill-in-blank Units 1–8
Sentence with a gap. User selects from 3–4 word tiles. Immediate right/wrong feedback. The only format used in every single unit — it scales from simple vocabulary to complex grammar.
Matching Units 2, 6
Two columns — Arabic on left, meanings on right. Drag lines to connect pairs. Score all at once. Used when a cluster of vocabulary needs to be consolidated before moving forward.
Word order Units 3–5, 8
Shuffled Arabic word tiles. Tap into correct sentence order. Tests grammar instinct — the user must feel what sounds right. This is where Nahw is practiced without being named.
Grammar parse ⭐ Units 4, 5, 7
Tap each word to label its grammatical role — فاعل، مفعول، مبتدا، خبر. The only format of its kind in any Arabic app. This is how Nahw actually gets learned — active labelling, not passive reading. Warsh's strongest differentiator.
Conversation builder Unit 8
Dialogue shown with a gap. User picks the correct reply from options. Feels like a real exchange. Used only in Unit 8 when the user is ready to produce language, not just recognise it.

Hidden grammar — the reveal map
The user never sees grammar terminology until after they have already learned it. The reveal is the reward, not the starting point.
Lessons 1–3 Unit 2
User thinks: "Learning this and that — pointing words"
اِسْمِ اِشَارَه + نکرہ + تنوین
Demonstrative pronouns, indefinite nouns, tanween
Reveal: "You used نکرہ 50 times without knowing it."
Lessons 4–6 Unit 3
User thinks: "Describing things — the book is new"
جُمْلَہ اِسْمِيَّہ — مُبْتَدَا + خَبَر
Nominal sentence — subject + predicate
Reveal: "You built Arabic sentences the way classical scholars do."
Lessons 7–9 Unit 4
User thinks: "Where things are — in, on, from, to"
حُرُوفِ جَار + اِعْرَابِ جَر
Prepositions + genitive case
Reveal: "You learned I'rab without a single grammar table."
Lessons 10–12 Unit 4
User thinks: "Whose things are — Muhammad's book"
مُرَكَّبِ اِضَافِي — مُضَاف + مُضَافٌ إِلَيْه
Possessive construction
Reveal: "مسجدِ رسول اللہ — you now know exactly why it's written this way."
Lessons 13–15 Unit 4–5
User thinks: "Calling people — ya Muhammad!"
حَرْفِ نِدَا + هَمْزَةُ الْوَصْل
Vocative particle + connecting hamza
Reveal: "بِسْمِ اللهِ — you now understand every single word."
Unit 7 milestone Quran unit
Full parse of سورۃ الفاتحہ — every word labelled

Reveal: "You can now parse every word of Surah Al-Fatiha. Scholars call this full I'rab. You just did it."
Content production rules — locked
Every lesson must have
Exactly one Quranic ayah in the Hook — genuinely relevant, never decorative
At least 3 different interaction formats in Practice
True/False exercise at least once to reset pacing
Grammar term named only in the Reveal — never before
Ustaad Noor tip connecting today's lesson to a specific ayah
Surah Al-Fatiha meter updated in the Close
Feedback language — locked
Correct: "بارك الله فيك" + one sentence explaining why the answer is right
Wrong: "Almost — let's look at this again" + correct answer shown immediately
Never: "Wrong!", "Oops!", "Incorrect!", "Try again!" — these shame and break flow
Chapter length targets
One lesson = 5–8 minutes total
One chapter = 4–6 lessons
One unit = 3–5 chapters
Total curriculum = 8 units
Grammar parse rules (Unit 4+)
Only introduced after the concept has appeared in at least 3 previous lessons passively
Labels shown in Arabic: فاعل، مفعول، مبتدا، خبر — with English/Urdu gloss below
First parse exercise is always on a previously seen sentence — never a new one
Hint available on first attempt — removed from second attempt onward
